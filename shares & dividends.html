01 · Foundations

What are Shares and Dividends?

A share is a unit of ownership in a company. When you buy shares, you become a part-owner and are entitled to a portion of the company's profit, called a dividend. This chapter looks at how to calculate income, investment, and the actual percentage return — the yield — on shares bought at par, at a premium, or at a discount.

Why does it matter?

Investing in the stock market is one of the most common ways people grow their savings. But the price you actually pay for a share (its Market Value) can differ significantly from the value printed on the certificate (its Face Value) — and dividend is always paid on the Face Value. Understanding how these interact is essential to comparing investment options sensibly.

Where it's used

  • Comparing dividend-paying stocks
  • Calculating returns on a portfolio
  • Understanding bonus & rights issues
  • Evaluating brokerage costs
  • Long-term financial literacy

12 · One Page

Quick Revision

Key Facts

Income: I = N × FV × r/100

Investment: Inv = N × MV

Yield%: (r × FV)/MV

Number of shares: N = Investment ÷ MV

Rules to Remember

Dividend is always on Face Value, never Market Value

MV > FV ⟹ premium; MV < FV ⟹ discount

Doubling MV (rate, FV fixed) halves the yield%

All Definitions & Rules (memorize this)

Exam Tips

  • Always find the Market Value first (adjust for premium/discount) before computing Investment or Number of Shares.
  • Income is always Rate × Face Value × Number of shares ÷ 100 — never substitute Market Value here.
  • When a question gives Investment and asks for income, find N first, then apply the income formula.
  • Yield% and the declared dividend rate are equal only when the share is trading exactly at par.

Formula Sheet

Shares & Dividends — Formula Sheet

ICSE Class 10 Mathematics

Core Formulas

Income: I = N·FV·r/100

Investment: Inv = N·MV

Yield%: (r·FV)/MV

Number of shares: N = Inv/MV

Market Value Rules

At par: MV = FV

At premium: MV > FV

At discount: MV < FV

MV = FV + premium, or MV = FV − discount

Rules of Thumb

Doubling N (FV, MV, rate fixed) → I & Inv both double, Yield% unchanged

Doubling MV (rate, FV fixed) → Yield% halves

Bonus shares → free, no fresh payment

Rights issue → priced below MV
