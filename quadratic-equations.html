01 · Foundations

What is a Quadratic Equation?

A quadratic equation is a polynomial equation of degree 2 — the highest power of the unknown x is 2. Its standard form is ax² + bx + c = 0, where a, b, c are real numbers and a ≠ 0. This chapter is about finding the values of x (the roots) that make the equation true, and understanding what those roots reveal.

Why does it matter?

Many real-world situations — the path of a thrown ball, the area of a rectangular field, the relationship between speed, distance, and time — turn into quadratic equations once you set up the algebra. Knowing how to solve them, and how to tell in advance whether real solutions even exist, is a core problem-solving skill.

Where it's used

  • Number and digit problems
  • Age-related word problems
  • Speed, distance, and time problems
  • Area and geometry problems
  • Work and time (pipes, taps) problems

12 · One Page

Quick Revision

Key Facts

Standard form: ax² + bx + c = 0 (a ≠ 0)

Discriminant: D = b² − 4ac

Quadratic formula: x = (−b ± √D)/2a

Sum = −b/a  ·  Product = c/a

Rules to Remember

D > 0 ⟹ real & distinct roots

D = 0 ⟹ real & equal roots

D < 0 ⟹ no real roots

All Definitions & Rules (memorize this)

Exam Tips

  • Always try factorization first; switch to the quadratic formula only when the equation doesn't factorize neatly.
  • When a question asks for "nature of roots", just compute D — you don't need to solve the equation at all.
  • For word problems, define the unknown clearly, form the equation carefully, and always reject roots that don't make sense in context (like negative time or length).
  • Remember the minus sign in the sum of roots formula (−b/a) — it's the single most common slip in this chapter.
