01 · Foundations

What is Reflection?

Reflection is a transformation that produces the mirror image of a point or figure across a fixed line — every point moves to a new position on the opposite side of that line, at exactly the same perpendicular distance, so the whole figure "flips" without changing its size or shape.

Why does it matter?

Reflection is the simplest of the geometric transformations, yet it is the building block for understanding symmetry, congruence, and later, rotations and translations. On the coordinate plane, each of the standard mirror lines — the x-axis, the y-axis, the origin, and lines like x = a or y = b — gives a simple algebraic rule connecting an object's coordinates to its image's coordinates, so reflection problems become pure calculation rather than careful drawing.

Where it's used

  • Mirror and symmetry designs in art and architecture
  • Computer graphics and animation (sprite flipping)
  • Optics — how light reflects off a plane surface
  • Testing whether a figure has a line of symmetry
  • Understanding congruent and invariant figures
  • Foundation for rotation and translation transformations

05 · Identity

Invariant Points

An invariant point is a point that reflects onto itself — this can only happen if the point already lies exactly on the mirror line. Slide the point Q below along the x-axis and watch its image coincide with it exactly, no matter where you place it.

Animated example

The point Q(x, 0) always lies on the x-axis, whatever value x takes. Reflecting Q in the x-axis only changes the sign of its y-coordinate — but since y is already 0, the sign change does nothing. Drag the slider to move Q and watch the image land exactly on top of Q every time.

Quick Revision

Key Facts

x-axis: P(x,y) → P′(x,−y)

y-axis: P(x,y) → P′(−x,y)

Origin: P(x,y) → P′(−x,−y)

Line y=x: P(x,y) → P′(y,x)

Rule Facts

Line x=a: P(x,y) → P′(2a−x, y)

Line y=b: P(x,y) → P′(x, 2b−y)

Invariant point ⟺ point lies on the mirror line

All Definitions & Rules (memorize this)

Exam Tips

  • Learn which single coordinate changes for each rule — reflection in the x-axis flips y, reflection in the y-axis flips x, and never the other way round.
  • For x = a or y = b, always use "2a − x" or "2b − y" — do not just flip the sign as if a or b were zero.
  • To check for an invariant point, plug the coordinates into the reflection rule and see if the image equals the original point.
  • Reflecting in the x-axis and then the y-axis (in either order) always gives the same result as reflecting once in the origin.
