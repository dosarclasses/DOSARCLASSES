01 · Foundations

What is a Geometric Construction?

A construction is a drawing made using only a ruler (for straight lines) and a compass (for arcs and circles) — no protractor, no measuring angles directly, no shortcuts. Every construction is really a sequence of small, provable geometric facts, chained together to build an exact figure.

Why does it matter?

A "construction" isn't just neat drawing — it's a proof you can see. Every arc you strike encodes a geometric fact (equal radii, equal distances, a right angle), so the final figure is provably exact, not just visually close. This chapter builds on properties of triangles and circles you already know, and turns them into tools you can draw with.

Where it's used

  • Engineering & architectural drawing
  • Surveying and map-making
  • Machine part and gear design
  • Scale models and blueprints
  • Circular design work (logos, wheels)
  • Bridge and truss geometry

Quick Revision

Key Facts

Tangent ⊥ radius at the point of contact

Tangent length: PT = √(OP² − r²)

Circumcenter: meeting point of perpendicular bisectors

Incenter: meeting point of angle bisectors

Ratio & Similarity Facts

Divide AB in ratio m:n → mark (m+n) equal parts

Similar triangle scale factor k: sides × k

Right triangle circumcenter = midpoint of hypotenuse

All Definitions & Rules (memorize this)

Exam Tips

  • Never erase your construction arcs — examiners specifically look for visible compass marks as proof of method.
  • For the ratio-division construction, always mark (m+n) divisions, not just m or n alone.
  • When constructing a similar triangle with scale factor greater than 1, extend the original side rather than trying to shrink it.
  • Label every point (O, P, T, etc.) clearly as you go — it makes both your construction and any later working much easier to follow.
