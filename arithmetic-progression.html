01 · Foundations

What is an Arithmetic Progression?

An Arithmetic Progression (AP) is a sequence of numbers in which every term after the first is obtained by adding a fixed number — the common difference — to the term before it. It's the mathematics of "steady, predictable change," whether that change is going up, down, or staying flat.

Why does it matter?

Anything that changes by the same fixed amount every step follows an AP — a salary that rises by a fixed increment each year, a stack of chairs with a fixed number added per row, a loan repaid in equal instalments. Once you spot the pattern, you can predict any term or find the total of any number of terms without listing them all out.

Where it's used

  • Simple interest growth per year
  • Seating rows in a stadium/theatre
  • Fixed monthly savings plans
  • Stacking patterns (bricks, logs)
  • Uniformly increasing distances
  • Depreciation with equal instalments

05 · Identity

The Sum Formula

S_n = n/2[2a + (n−1)d]. It comes from Gauss's trick — write the series forwards and backwards, add the two, and every pair sums to the same value. Drag the slider below to change n and watch the sum grow.

Animated example

Base AP: a = 4, d = 3. Drag the slider to change how many terms (n) you're summing, and watch S_n update using the formula.

n = 6 last term = 19 S_n = 69
Check S_6 = 6/2 × (4+19) = 3 × 23 = 69 — pairing the first and last term always gives the same sum.

12 · One Page

Quick Revision

Key Facts

nth term: a_n = a + (n−1)d

Common difference: d = a_(n+1) − a_n

Arithmetic mean: A = (a+b)/2

Three numbers in AP: a−d, a, a+d

Sum Facts

S_n = n/2[2a + (n−1)d]

S_n = n/2(a + l)

a_n = S_n − S_(n−1)

All Definitions & Rules (memorize this)

Exam Tips

  • Always find a and d first — almost every AP problem reduces to finding these two values.
  • When a problem gives you two terms, subtract their equations to eliminate a and solve for d first.
  • If you know S_n as a formula in n, get a_n via a_n = S_n − S_(n−1), and remember a_1 = S_1 separately.
  • For "three/four numbers in AP" problems, always represent them symmetrically (like a−d, a, a+d) to keep the algebra simple.
