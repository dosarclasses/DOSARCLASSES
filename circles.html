01 · Foundations

What is a Circle?

A circle is the set of all points in a plane that are the same fixed distance — the radius — from a fixed point, the center. Nearly every result in this chapter is really a statement about angles: how they relate to arcs, chords, and tangents, no matter where a point sits on the circle.

Why does it matter?

Circle theorems let you find unknown angles and lengths without measuring anything directly — a single fact, like a central angle or one tangent length, unlocks every other angle or length connected to it. This "one clue, whole picture" idea is what makes circle geometry so useful in construction, design, and exam problems alike.

Where it's used

  • Wheels, gears & rotating machinery
  • Arches, domes & architecture
  • Satellite & planetary orbits
  • Lenses & optical design
  • Road curves & roundabout design
  • GPS triangulation & navigation

02 · The Instrument

Parts of a Circle

Every theorem in this chapter is built from these parts. Hover a label below, or the diagram itself, to see exactly which piece is which — precise vocabulary is half the battle in circle geometry.

Radius — a segment from the center to any point on the circle.
Diameter — a chord through the center; the longest chord, equal to 2×radius.
Chord — a segment joining any two points on the circle.
Arc — a part of the circumference, cut off by two points.
Sector — the region between two radii and the arc between them.
Segment — the region between a chord and its arc.
Tangent — a line touching the circle at exactly one point.

06 · Identity

Cyclic Quadrilaterals

A quadrilateral is cyclic when all four vertices lie on one circle. Whenever that happens, each pair of opposite angles is supplementary — they always add to 180°.

Animated example

Drag the slider to change ∠A of cyclic quadrilateral ABCD. Watch ∠C update instantly so the pair always sums to 180°.

∠A = 80° ∠C = 100°
Check ∠A + ∠C = 80° + 100° = 180° — always true for opposite angles of a cyclic quadrilateral.

Key Facts

Angles in same segment are equal

Tangent Facts

Tangent ⟂ radius at point of contact

Two tangents from a point are equal

Tangent-chord ∠ = ∠ in alternate segment

All Theorems (memorize this)

Exam Tips

  • Always draw the radius to the point of tangency — it creates a right angle you can use immediately.
  • Check whether an angle is at the center or the circumference before applying the 2:1 rule.
  • In cyclic quadrilateral problems, only opposite angles are supplementary — adjacent angles are not, in general.
  • Remember that two tangents from the same external point are always equal, before reaching for trigonometry.
