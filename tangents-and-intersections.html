01 · Foundations

Tangents, Secants, and Their Intersections

A tangent touches a circle at exactly one point; a secant cuts through it at two. This chapter is about the elegant, predictable relationships that appear whenever lines like these meet a circle — or meet each other, inside or outside it.

Why does it matter?

Whether two chords cross inside a circle, two secants cross outside it, or a tangent and a secant meet at the same external point — the segments they create are always linked by a simple multiplication rule. Once you can spot which configuration you're looking at, the rest is arithmetic.

Where it's used

  • Finding unknown lengths in circle diagrams
  • Proving angle relationships in cyclic figures
  • Constructing tangents to a given circle
  • Understanding how circles touch one another
  • Foundations for coordinate geometry of circles

12 · One Page

Quick Revision

Key Facts

Tangent length: PT = √(d² − r²)

Intersecting chords: PA × PB = PC × PD

Intersecting secants: PA × PB = PC × PD

Tangent-secant: PT² = PA × PB

Rules to Remember

Tangents from one external point are equal

Angle AOB = 180° − angle APB

Tangent-chord angle = angle in alternate segment

All Definitions & Rules (memorize this)

Exam Tips

  • First identify the configuration — chords inside, secants outside, or a tangent with a secant — before choosing a formula.
  • Remember that "PB" in a secant always means the WHOLE length from P to the far point, not just the chord between the two circle points.
  • For tangent-secant problems, always square the tangent length: PT² = PA × PB, never PT alone.
  • When comparing the angle between two tangents and the angle at the center, remember they are supplementary (add to 180°), not equal.
