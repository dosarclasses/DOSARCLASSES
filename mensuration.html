01 · Foundations

What is Mensuration?

Mensuration is the branch of mathematics concerned with measuring geometric figures — their lengths, areas and volumes. This chapter extends the two-dimensional measurement of circles into three dimensions, covering the cylinder, cone, sphere, hemisphere and frustum.

Why does it matter?

Every solid object around us — a water tank, an ice-cream cone, a ball, a bucket — can be approximated by one of a handful of standard shapes. Once you know the surface area and volume formulas for these shapes, you can find how much material is needed to build something, how much liquid it can hold, or what happens when one solid is melted and reshaped into another.

Where it's used

  • Finding the capacity of tanks, buckets and silos
  • Estimating material needed for construction
  • Melting and recasting metals into new shapes
  • Packaging and manufacturing design
  • Calculating paint or canvas required to cover a surface
  • Engineering and architectural design

Melting, Recasting & Conservation of Volume

When a solid is melted down and recast into a different shape, its volume never changes — only its surface area and dimensions do. Slide the radius of the new cylinder below and watch its height adjust automatically to keep the volume locked to the original sphere.

12 · One Page

Quick Revision

Key Facts

Cylinder: CSA = 2πrh, TSA = 2πr(h+r), V = πr²h

Cone: l = √(r²+h²), CSA = πrl, V = (1/3)πr²h

Sphere: SA = 4πr², V = (4/3)πr³

Rule Facts

Hemisphere: CSA = 2πr², TSA = 3πr², V = (2/3)πr³

Frustum: V = (1/3)πh(r₁²+r₂²+r₁r₂)

Melting & recasting: volume before = volume after

All Definitions & Rules (memorize this)

Exam Tips

  • Always check whether the diameter or the radius has been given — halve the diameter before substituting into any formula.
  • Never confuse the slant height (l) with the vertical height (h) of a cone; volume always needs the vertical height.
  • In melting-and-recasting problems, equate volumes, never surface areas — the material is conserved, not the outer skin.
  • Keep units consistent throughout a question; convert everything to the same unit before applying a formula.
