01 · Foundations

What is Probability?

Probability is a number between 0 and 1 that measures how likely an event is to happen. It turns everyday phrases like "unlikely", "even chance" and "certain" into precise fractions, by comparing the number of outcomes we want against the total number of equally likely outcomes possible.

Why does it matter?

Once an experiment's outcomes are known to be equally likely, every question about chance becomes a counting problem — count the favourable outcomes, count the total outcomes, and divide. This classical definition is the foundation for every later idea in probability, from weather forecasting and insurance to genetics and quality control, and at the ICSE level it is applied to simple, everyday experiments: coins, dice, cards, and bags of coloured balls.

Where it's used

  • Weather forecasts ("70% chance of rain")
  • Games of chance — cards, dice, lotteries
  • Insurance and risk assessment
  • Quality control and sampling in factories
  • Genetics and inherited traits
  • Sports statistics and match predictions

05 · Identity

Complementary Events

However you split a sample space into an event E and its complement "not E", the two probabilities always add up to exactly 1. Slide below to change how many balls in a bag of 10 are red, and watch P(red) and P(not red) move — but their sum never budges from 1.

Animated example

A bag has 10 balls in total; x of them are red and the rest are not. P(red) = x/10 and P(not red) = (10−x)/10. Drag the slider to change x and watch the two probabilities trade off, while their sum stays locked at exactly 1.

Red balls x = 3 P(red) + P(not red) = 1
Check P(red) = 3/10, P(not red) = 7/10, sum = 3/10 + 7/10 = 1 — however x changes, the two probabilities always add up to exactly 1.

Quick Revision

Key Facts

P(E) = n(E) / n(S)

0 ≤ P(E) ≤ 1

Sure event: P(E) = 1

Impossible event: P(E) = 0

Rule Facts

P(E) + P(not E) = 1

Deck of cards: 52 total, 26 red, 26 black

Face cards: 12 (J, Q, K in each suit)

All Definitions & Rules (memorize this)

Exam Tips

  • Always write the sample space (or at least count n(S) carefully) before counting favourable outcomes — it prevents silly counting errors.
  • For "not E" questions, it is often faster to find P(E) first and subtract from 1, rather than counting the complement directly.
  • Memorise the deck-of-cards facts (52 total, 4 suits of 13, 12 face cards, 4 of each rank) — they appear in almost every card-based question.
  • Always simplify your final probability fraction to its lowest terms, and double check it lies between 0 and 1.
