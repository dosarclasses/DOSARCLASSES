01 · Foundations

What is a Locus?

A locus (plural: loci) is the path traced out by a point as it moves according to a stated geometrical rule. Instead of finding one point, a locus problem asks you to describe the whole set of positions a point could occupy — usually a straight line, a pair of lines, or a circle.

Why does it matter?

Locus thinking turns a vague description ("a point that stays equally far from two corners") into a precise, drawable shape. It underlies compass-and-ruler constructions, coordinate geometry, and even real-world problems like finding a site equidistant from two towns or a lamppost equidistant from two roads.

Where it's used

  • Geometric constructions with compass and ruler
  • Finding a triangle's circumcentre or incentre
  • Coordinate geometry equations of curves
  • Surveying and site-planning problems
  • Combining two conditions to pin down a point
  • Understanding circles, parabolas and conics as loci

05 · Identity

Combining Two Loci

Most exam problems don't stop at one condition — they ask for a point satisfying two conditions at once. You find each locus separately, then take their point(s) of intersection. Scale the example below by any factor k — because PA and PB are always equal on the perpendicular bisector, that equality never breaks, whatever k you choose.

Animated example

P lies on the perpendicular bisector of AB, so by definition PA = PB. Drag the slider to imagine scaling the whole figure by k — PA and PB both scale together, and stay exactly equal, which is exactly why any circle you intersect this locus with meets it symmetrically about AB.

Quick Revision

Key Facts

Equidistant from a point ⟹ Circle

Equidistant from 2 points ⟹ Perpendicular bisector

Equidistant from 2 lines ⟹ Angle bisector(s)

Rule Facts

Fixed distance from a line ⟹ 2 parallel lines

Equidistant from 2 parallel lines ⟹ midway line

Circumcentre = ⊥ bisectors; Incentre = angle bisectors

All Definitions & Theorems (memorize this)

Exam Tips

  • Always state both the shape and the exact measurements when describing a locus — "a circle" alone isn't enough; give the radius and centre too.
  • When two conditions are combined, find each locus separately first, then look for their intersection — don't try to solve both conditions in one step.
  • Remember Pythagoras' theorem whenever a problem combines "equidistant from two points" with "a fixed distance from one of them."
  • For coordinate-geometry locus questions, start from PA² = PB² (or the given distance condition) and simplify fully before stating the final equation.

Point & Line Loci

Fixed point ⟹ circle (centre = point, radius = distance)

Two points ⟹ perpendicular bisector of the segment

Fixed line ⟹ 2 lines parallel to it, at the given distance

Angle & Combined Loci

Two intersecting lines ⟹ pair of angle bisectors (⊥ to each other)

Two parallel lines ⟹ 1 line, midway between them

Combined condition ⟹ intersection of the two individual loci

Triangle Centres & Special Curves

Circumcentre = equidistant from vertices (∩ of ⊥ bisectors)

Incentre = equidistant from sides (∩ of angle bisectors)

∠APB = 90° ⟹ circle with AB as diameter

PA = k·PB (k≠1) ⟹ Apollonius circle
