01 · Foundations

What are Heights and Distances?

Heights and Distances is the practical application of trigonometry: using an observed angle — of elevation or depression — together with a known length, to work out a height or a distance that can't be measured directly. Every problem in this chapter reduces to the same shape: a right-angled triangle.

Why does it matter?

You can't stretch a measuring tape to the top of a tower or across a river — but you can measure an angle with a simple instrument (a clinometer) and a distance along the ground. Trigonometric ratios turn that angle and distance into the height or distance you actually need.

Where it's used

  • Measuring the height of towers, buildings, hills
  • Navigation and surveying at sea and on land
  • Finding the width of a river without crossing it
  • Aviation and radar angle calculations
  • Combining two observations to pin down one unknown
  • Classic "cloud and lake" and "ladder" problems

05 · Identity

Combining Two Observations

Many exam problems give two angles of elevation from two different points, in line with the object. You set up two tan equations sharing the same unknown height, then solve them together. Scale the classic 30°/60° example below by any factor k — the ratio between height and near-distance never changes, because it's always tan60°.

Animated example

From two points on the same side of a tower, 20k m apart, the angles of elevation are 30° and 60°. Solving the two triangles together gives the near distance and the height in terms of k. Drag the slider to scale the whole figure by k, and watch both values scale together, since the triangles stay similar.

Near distance = 10k = 10 m Height = 10√3k ≈ 17.32 m
Check Height ÷ Near distance = 1.732 (= tan60°) — always tan60°, no matter what k is.

Quick Revision

Key Facts

Angle of elevation ⟹ measured upward from horizontal

Angle of depression ⟹ measured downward from horizontal

Height = Base × tanθ

Rule Facts

Base = Height ÷ tanθ

Line of Sight = Height ÷ sinθ = Base ÷ cosθ

Depression from top = Elevation from bottom (alternate angles)

All Definitions & Formulas (memorize this)

Exam Tips

  • Always draw a clearly labelled right-angled triangle first, marking the angle, height, base and line of sight, before choosing a trig ratio.
  • Remember the angle of depression is measured from the observer's horizontal, not from the vertical drop — a common source of errors.
  • In two-point (combined) problems, set up both tanθ equations in terms of the same unknown height, then eliminate one variable algebraically.
  • Keep answers in exact surd form (like 10√3) until the final step, and only convert to a decimal at the end, to avoid rounding errors.
