01 · Foundations

What is Similarity?

Two figures are similar when they have exactly the same shape — their corresponding angles are equal and their corresponding sides are proportional — even if their sizes are different. Similarity is the mathematics behind scale models, maps, photographs, and shadows.

Why does it matter?

Similarity lets us relate the measurements of a small, easy-to-measure figure to a large, hard-to-measure one — a stick and its shadow, a model and a real building, a map and actual land. Once two triangles are known to be similar, every ratio of corresponding sides, perimeters and areas follows from a single scale factor.

Where it's used

  • Maps and scale drawings
  • Finding heights using shadows
  • Architectural and engineering models
  • Enlarging or reducing photographs
  • Right-triangle altitude relationships
  • Proving geometric identities

05 · Identity

Areas of Similar Triangles

When every side of a triangle is scaled by k, its perimeter also scales by k — but its area scales by k², because area depends on two dimensions at once. Scale the reference triangle below by any factor k and watch the difference.

Animated example

Triangle ABC has sides 3 cm, 4 cm and 5 cm, so its perimeter is 12 cm and its area is 6 cm². Drag the slider to scale every side by k to form the similar Triangle PQR, and watch the perimeter scale by k while the area scales by k².

Perimeter of ABC = 12 = 12 Perimeter of PQR = 12k = 12
Check Area of ABC = 6 cm², Area of PQR = 6 × 1² = 6 cm² — the area always scales by k², never by k.

All Definitions & Rules (memorize this)

Exam Tips

  • Always name similar triangles with vertices in corresponding order — △ABC ~ △PQR means A↔P, B↔Q, C↔R.
  • For areas, square the side ratio; for perimeters, medians and altitudes, use the side ratio directly — never mix the two up.
  • Before applying the Basic Proportionality Theorem, confirm the line really is parallel to a side of the triangle.
  • In right-triangle altitude problems, redraw the three triangles separately (original, and the two smaller ones) to avoid matching up the wrong sides.
